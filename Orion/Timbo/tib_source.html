TIMBÓ SOURCE

 

UNDULATOR - CPMU14

TIMBÓ is an Insertion Device (ID) beamline, located at the low-beta straight-section in sector 04. This source point allows TIMBÓ to obtain the maximum coherence that Sirius can deliver. Due to the need of maximizing the flux at the sample, the best source option for TIMBÓ beamline is a Cryogenic Permanent Magnet Undulator (CPMU) with a short period.

The CPMU has its magnets cooled down by LN2 (in-vacuum magnets) and its operational temperature is 80 K. The cryogenic temperature increases the remanent field of the magnets material and allows the undulator to achieve stronger magnetic fields at the gap center. For this reason, higher deflection values (K) can be achieved at the same magnetic gap. The low temperature of the magnets also makes them more resistant to demagnetization, since this effect occurs when the temperature rises above a certain point. In storage rings, the temperature of undulator magnets can increase basically due to two mechanisms: the loss of electrons that collide with the magnets and due to radiation absorption by the magnets. Both effects are worsened as the gap value decreases. Since for TIMBÓ we plan to operate the undulator at gaps below 4 mm, the demagnetization risk reduction due to cryogenic temperatures is an important feature of CPMUs for Sirius. 

This section describes the TIMBÓ undulator parameters and its light properties. The results below were calculated using SPECTRA v11.2 (spectrax.org), using the rigorous Wigner function methods.

Note that the maximum K-value achieved does not satisfy the energy continuity requirement (which happens for K ≥ 2), and therefore there is a gap between the 1st and 3rd harmonic (from 6 to 7 keV). In principle, this energy gap could be bridged by using the 2nd harmonic, but if the downstream optics aperture is too small, this harmonic will deliver much lower flux on-axis. To avoid this gap, the undulator period could be increased, however this would result in a lower flux for all energies. LIGHT PROPERTIES

Brilliance

Brilliance is the most used figure-of-merit to describe the spectral performance of an X-ray source. It is defined as the source flux within a small bandwidth (0.1%) divided by the source emittance. The values were normalized to a storage ring current of 100 mA (the brilliance scales linearly with the current). Therefore, it tells us how many photons are emitted in a small area and divergence.

The small period was optimized for achieving high brilliance at the highest energy of the beamline, considering the relevant constraints such as the Beam Stay Clear (BSC). Figure 3.1 shows the brilliance as function of the radiation energy. In this graph, each curve represents an odd harmonic (starting from h=1), as the gap is scanned from minimum to maximum. The TIMBÓ undulator will be Sirius' most brilliant source point for this energy range, due to the combination of small βx,βy values and the powerful CPMU14.  

 

Flux

The spectral flux for TIMBÓ beamline was calculated inside the capillary mirror acceptance: a circular aperture with a diameter of 11 µrad, and a central beamstop with a diameter of 6 µrad. This corresponds to the capillary diameters D1 = 0.8 mm and D2 = 1.4 mm at a position 117 m downstream the source point. Therefore, it can be used as a reference to calculate the amount of flux delivered to the experimental station. Is calculated as the number of photons per second inside a small bandwidth (0.1%), at the storage ring of 100 mA (the flux scales linearly with the current).

Figure 3.3 shows the flux density (in units of flux divided by area) at 117 m from the source, for the energy E = 12 keV. The dotted rectangle depicts the capillary mirror aperture. It can be seen that such an aperture is smaller than the beam at this position. 

 

 

 

Source Size and Divergence

The source size of TIMBÓ is nearly constant for E > 6 keV, with horizontal size dominated by the electron beam dimension, and vertical size dominated by the diffraction limit (see Figure 3.4). Thus, in most energies the photon beam sizes are equal to σx = 20 µm and σy = 5 µm (source aspect ratio = 4). The rate of how much the beam size grows with the distance is determined by the beam divergence.  As one can see in Figure 3.5, the divergence fluctuates around the value of σ'x = 15 µrad and σ'y = 8 µrad (except for the first harmonic). 

 

 

Power Load

The power load was calculated inside a rectangular aperture of 60 x 60 µrad², which is the typical frontend aperture, for the maximum storage ring current I = 350 mA. The maximum power load occurs at the maximum value for the deflection parameter Kmax = 1.79, and is equal to Pmax = 203.7 W. The peak power density is 59.5 kW/mrad². To calculate the power density in W/mm², one can simply divide this value by the square of the distance from the source zsrc. For example, at zsrc = 30 m, the power density is 66.1 W/mm². Figure 3.6 shows the power density distribution for such conditions, with a rectangle displaying the frontend aperture size.

 

 

Polarization

The degree of polarization was calculated at the capillary mirror aperture, as the flux. Figure 3.7 shows the degree of linear polarization (PL²) and the degree of circular polarization (PC²). These values are defined in terms of the Stokes parameters: PL = S1/S0 and PC = S3/S0. As one can see, the radiation inside such aperture is highly linearly polarized.
