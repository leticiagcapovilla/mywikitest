HIBISCO SOURCE

 

CPMU14

HIBISCO is an Insertion Device (ID) beamline, located at the high-beta straight-section in sector 05. This source point allows that HIBISCO experimental station is as close as possible to where the living samples will be located. Due to the high energy range reaching up to 45 keV, the best source option for HIBISCO beamline is a Cryogenic Permanent Magnet Undulator (CPMU) with a short period. This section describes the HIBISCO undulator parameters and its light properties. The results below were calculated using SPECTRA v11.2 (spectrax.org), using the rigorous Wigner function methods.

The CPMU has its magnets cooled down by LN2 (in-vacuum magnets) and its operational temperature is 80 K. The cryogenic temperature increases the remanent field of the magnets material and allows the undulator to achieve stronger magnetic fields at the gap center. For this reason, higher deflection values (K) can be achieved at the same magnetic gap. The low temperature of the magnets also makes them more resistant to demagnetization, since this effect occurs when the temperature rises above a certain point. In storage rings, the temperature of undulator magnets can increase basically due to two mechanisms: the loss of electrons that collide with the magnets and due to radiation absorption by the magnets. Both effects are worsened as the gap value decreases. Since for HIBISCO we plan to operate the undulator at gaps below 5 mm, the demagnetization risk reduction due to cryogenic temperatures is an important feature of CPMUs for Sirius.

LIGHT PROPERTIES

Brilliance

Brilliance is the most used figure-of-merit to describe the spectral performance of an X-ray source. It is defined as the source flux within a small bandwidth (0.1%) divided by the source emittance. The values were normalized to a storage ring current of 100 mA (the brilliance scales linearly with the current). Therefore, it tells us how many photons are emitted in a small area and divergence.

The small period was optimized for achieving high brilliance at the highest energy of the beamline, considering the relevant constraints such as the Beam Stay Clear (BSC). Figure 3.1 shows the brilliance as function of the radiation energy. In this graph, each curve represents an odd harmonic (starting from h=1), as the gap is scanned from minimum to maximum.   

 

 

Flux

The spectral flux for HIBISCO beamline was calculated inside an aperture of 20 x 20 µrad², which is close to the optical system acceptance, and shown in Figure 3.2. Therefore, it can be used as a reference to calculate the amount of flux delivered to the experimental station. Is calculated as the number of photons per second inside a small bandwidth (0.1%), at the storage ring of 100 mA (the flux scales linearly with the current).

Figure 3.3 shows the flux density (in units of flux divided by area) at 45 m from the source, for the energy E = 38 keV. The dotted rectangle depicts the aperture of 20 x 20 µrad, which at this position, corresponds to 0.9 x 0.9 mm². It can be seen that such an aperture is sufficient to collect most of the beam intensity. 

 

Source Size and Divergence

The source size of HIBISCO is nearly constant for E > 15 keV, with sizes dominated by the electron beam dimensions. Thus, in most energies the photon beam sizes are equal to \sigma_x = 65.3 µm and \sigma_y = 3.0 µm. As in other high-beta beamlines, the beam is highly asymmetric at the source point, being much larger in the horizontal plane. However, as it propagates a few meters away from the source (leaving the Rayleigh range), it becomes a nearly round beam (see Figure 3.3). The rate of how much the beam size grows with the distance is determined by the beam divergence. As one can see in Figure 3.4, the divergence fluctuates around the value of sigma'_x = 8.5 µrad and sigma'_y = 7.5 µrad.

 

Power Load

The power load was calculated inside a rectangular aperture of 60 x 60 µrad², which is the typical frontend aperture, for the maximum storage ring current I = 350 mA. The maximum power load occurs at the maximum value for the deflection parameter Kmax = 1.41, and is equal to Pmax = 170.6 W. The peak power density is 46.5 kW/mrad². To calculate the power density in W/mm², one can simply divide this value by the square of the distance from the source zsrc. For example, at zsrc = 30 m, the power density is 51.7 W/mm². Figure 3.5 shows the power density distribution for such conditions, with a rectangle displaying the aperture size.

 

 

Polarization

The degree of polarization was calculated at an aperture of 20 x 20 µrad². Figure 3.6 shows the degree of linear polarization (PL²) and the degree of circular polarization (PC²). These values are defined in terms of the Stokes parameters: PL = S1/S0 and PC = S3/S0. As one can see, the radiation inside such aperture is highly linearly polarized.
